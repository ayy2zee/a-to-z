Gunboats to Rejuvenation: How the "Century of Humiliation" Became the PRC's Master Narrative

Part 1 of 5 — The Century of Humiliation and Chinese Government Policy

When Xi Jinping introduced the "Chinese Dream" in late 2012, he chose a setting that revealed as much about the Chinese state's worldview as the speech itself. He did not unveil the idea at an economic summit or a Party school seminar. He did it at the National Museum of China, inside an exhibition called "The Road to Rejuvenation." That choice was deliberate. It framed China's future not simply as a policy program, but as the endpoint of a historical journey: from invasion and weakness to unity and strength, from national humiliation to national revival.

That framing is more than rhetoric. It helps explain how the People's Republic of China understands its own mission.

The "Century of Humiliation" is often used as shorthand for the long period between the Opium War and the founding of the PRC in 1949—a century marked by foreign invasion, unequal treaties, territorial loss, civil war, and political fragmentation. But in contemporary Chinese politics, it is not just a historical period. It is a governing story. It is a curated narrative about what went wrong, why China became vulnerable, and what must never be allowed to happen again.

In official tellings, the story begins when Western imperial powers "forced open China's door with gunboats." China, so the standard formulation goes, was then reduced to a "semi-colonial and semi-feudal society." The turning point comes only with the rise of the Chinese Communist Party and, ultimately, the founding of the PRC. In this account, 1949 was not just a regime change. It was the moment China "stood up."

This is why the "Century of Humiliation" matters so much. It is not merely how the state remembers the past. It is how the state justifies the present.

History and Memory Politics

To understand that, it helps to separate history from memory politics. The underlying events are real: the Opium Wars, the cession of Hong Kong, foreign spheres of influence, the Boxer indemnity, the Japanese invasion, the collapse of the Qing, and the long disorder that followed. But the significance of those events in modern Chinese politics lies in how they are arranged into a lesson.

That lesson is clear and consistent. China was weak, and weakness invited domination. China was divided, and division invited exploitation. China lagged behind, and backwardness made it vulnerable. Rejuvenation, then, requires the opposite: unity, strength, development, and a political center capable of defending sovereignty.

Xi said this directly in his 2012 museum speech. He tied the "Chinese Dream" to "our struggles in the over 170 years since the Opium War," explicitly anchoring the present to a historical timeline that begins in foreign coercion. Then he offered a sentence that may be the clearest summary of the Party's governing logic: "Backwardness left us vulnerable to attack, whereas only development makes us strong."

That line deserves to be read carefully, because it does a great deal of work.

It does not present development as a normal economic objective, the kind pursued by any government hoping to raise incomes. It presents development as the remedy for historical trauma. Prosperity is not just about material well-being. It is about ensuring that China can never again be pushed around by stronger powers. In that sense, development becomes inseparable from security, and security becomes inseparable from history.

Rejuvenation as Historical Burden

This is one reason the language of "national rejuvenation" is so central to the PRC's political vocabulary. Rejuvenation is not simply modernization. It is modernization with a historical burden attached to it. It means recovering China's strength after a period of weakness, restoring dignity after a period of subordination, and completing a national mission that the Party claims earlier generations were unable to achieve.

That mission is repeated constantly in official texts. The 2011 white paper China's Peaceful Development begins with the now familiar story of Western gunboats and national decline. Xi's centenary speech in 2021 repeats the same arc, again describing modern China's starting point as a "semi-colonial, semi-feudal society," and again presenting the Party as the force that ended national humiliation. In one of the most quoted lines from that speech, Xi declared: "We will never allow any foreign force to bully, oppress, or subjugate us."

That is not just patriotic flourish. It is a statement of doctrine, rooted in memory.

The Party's 2021 historical resolution reinforces this structure even further. It presents the Party's history as a continuous movement from revolution to prosperity to strength—from "standing up," to "growing prosperous," to "becoming strong." The point is not merely to celebrate national achievement. It is to cast the Party as the vehicle through which history itself has been corrected. In that telling, the Party did not simply win power. It rescued China from the conditions that made humiliation possible.

And because that claim sits at the heart of the Party's legitimacy, it has to be reproduced constantly and institutionally. It cannot live only in speeches.

Narrative Infrastructure

That is where museums, textbooks, patriotic education, commemorations, and official publications come in. The National Museum's "Road to Rejuvenation" exhibition is especially important because it turns a state narrative into a physical experience. Visitors do not just read about history; they move through a staged account of national decline and recovery, one that culminates in the present order. The exhibition is not a temporary political message. It is permanent narrative infrastructure.

Scholars of Chinese nationalism have been making this point for years. William Callahan has described national humiliation as a master narrative of modern Chinese identity, one that often takes the form of a movement "from humiliation to glory." Zheng Wang, in his work on patriotic education and historical memory, argues that the injunction to "never forget national humiliation" became a foundational part of post-1989 political education. Alison Adcock Kaufman has shown how this same historical memory shapes elite thinking about international order and China's place within it.

Taken together, these insights help clarify what the "Century of Humiliation" does in practice. It is not just a story about the past. It is a way of organizing threat perception in the present.

Organizing Threat Perception

A state that sees its modern history through the lens of humiliation is likely to treat certain risks as uniquely serious. Territorial division is not just a constitutional issue; it becomes a replay of national dismemberment. Foreign pressure is not just diplomacy; it can be interpreted as the possible return of coercion. Ideological drift is not just debate; it may appear as a threat to the unity that the official narrative says was necessary for national survival. Once those associations are established, they help explain why sovereignty is treated with such intensity, why political cohesion is prized so highly, and why the Party insists on linking development with security.

This does not mean history mechanically determines policy. China's behavior can also be explained in more conventional ways. Realists will point to power transitions, geography, and strategic competition. Political economists will emphasize industrial policy, state capacity, and the demands of long-run development. Institutionalists will focus on the logic of Leninist party rule. All of those explanations matter.

But they do not make the humiliation narrative irrelevant. They miss something important if they treat it as mere propaganda or symbolic decoration.

The "Century of Humiliation" matters because it supplies the legitimating logic through which policy is narrated and defended. It gives the state a ready-made explanation for why strength is necessary, why sovereignty is non-negotiable, and why the Party must remain at the center of national life. It connects domestic governance to civilizational recovery. It transforms policy priorities into historical obligations.

That is why the narrative remains so durable. It is useful. It explains the past, organizes the present, and justifies the future—all at once.

History as Infrastructure

Seen this way, the PRC's historical narrative is not ornamental. It is infrastructural. It tells citizens what the country has suffered, what the Party has supposedly repaired, and what dangers still lurk in the world beyond China's borders. It defines weakness not just as an unfortunate condition, but as a moral and political failure with catastrophic consequences. And it defines rejuvenation not simply as national success, but as the prevention of history's return.

That is the larger claim this essay series will explore. The "Century of Humiliation" is not just a background reference in Chinese politics. It is one of the main frameworks through which the Party-state interprets risk, legitimizes authority, and links security to development.

In the essays that follow, I'll look at how this narrative is reproduced through education and public memory, and how it travels from symbolic history into concrete doctrine—especially in questions of sovereignty, security, and the state's understanding of external pressure.
