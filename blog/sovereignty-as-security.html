Sovereignty as Security: Taiwan, Territorial Integrity, and Military Modernization After "Humiliation"

For the Chinese Communist Party, sovereignty is not just a legal principle. It is the lesson drawn from a century of invasion, partition, and coercion. That helps explain why Beijing treats Taiwan, territorial integrity, and military modernization not as ordinary policy questions, but as tests of whether China has truly escaped the vulnerabilities of its past.

Part 3 of 5 — The Century of Humiliation and Chinese Government Policy

In the official language of the People's Republic of China, few phrases carry more political force than "sovereignty" and "territorial integrity." These are not just diplomatic formulas. They function as red lines—markers of whether China is secure, respected, and fully restored after what it calls the "Century of Humiliation."1

That framing matters because it reveals a core mechanism in modern Chinese policy. The historical lesson the CCP draws from the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries is that a weak and divided China invited foreign coercion. A strong and unified China, by contrast, can prevent the return of that condition. In that sense, sovereignty is not merely a legal claim. It is a security doctrine.

This doctrine is most visible where Beijing is least flexible: Taiwan, territorial integrity, and national defense. Across official laws, white papers, and leadership speeches, the same pattern appears. Division is treated as intolerable, foreign involvement is treated as containment, and military modernization is treated as necessary insurance against renewed subjugation.2

The lesson of humiliation: never divided, never vulnerable again

The "Century of Humiliation" occupies a foundational place in the PRC's political memory. It is remembered as the era in which China was invaded, forced into unequal treaties, stripped of autonomy, and denied equality in the international order. In official narratives, the trauma is not only that China lost wars. It is that national weakness opened the door to repeated external interference.3

That historical memory helps explain why modern Chinese policy treats sovereignty questions with such absolutism. If the central lesson of humiliation is that weakness allowed outsiders to carve into China's territory and politics, then preventing any new division becomes more than a matter of nationalism. It becomes a matter of regime legitimacy and civilizational restoration.

Alison Adcock Kaufman has argued that elite Chinese debates about international competition and equality are still shaped by this historical inheritance. The debates that emerged during the humiliation era, she writes, "underpin similar elite debates" in China today.4 That is a useful way of thinking about the relationship between memory and policy. The past is not just commemorated. It supplies the categories through which the present is interpreted: competition, containment, inequality, and vulnerability.

This helps explain why the PRC does not speak about sovereignty in the abstract. Sovereignty is framed as the opposite of humiliation. Territorial integrity is framed as proof that China has escaped the fate of being partitioned or dictated to by stronger powers. And the state's most sensitive external disputes are cast as unfinished business in that long historical recovery.

Taiwan as the sharpest test of national restoration

No issue better illustrates this logic than Taiwan.

The legal foundation is the 2005 Anti-Secession Law, which sets out Beijing's position in unambiguous terms. Article 1 states that the law exists to oppose and check secession, promote national reunification, preserve "China's sovereignty and territorial integrity," and safeguard "the fundamental interests of the Chinese nation."5 Article 2 goes further, declaring that "China's sovereignty and territorial integrity brook no division."6

The importance of this language is not simply legal. It reveals how Beijing conceptualizes the Taiwan question. Taiwan is not treated as a negotiable political disagreement between two sides with competing claims to legitimacy. It is framed as a threat to the unity of the nation itself. Division is presented not as an unresolved status quo, but as an unacceptable wound in the national body.

That framing hardened even further in the 2022 white paper, The Taiwan Question and China's Reunification in the New Era. In its preamble, the paper states that complete reunification is "indispensable for the realization of China's rejuvenation."7 It also claims that "external forces have tried to exploit Taiwan to contain China" and to halt the process of national rejuvenation.8

Those two claims, taken together, are especially revealing. First, reunification is linked directly to rejuvenation. Taiwan is not just a territorial issue; it is presented as a condition for China's national revival. Second, foreign involvement in Taiwan is not interpreted as ordinary great-power competition. It is cast as a continuation of a historical pattern in which outside powers exploit Chinese weakness to obstruct China's rise.

This is where the "Century of Humiliation → modern policy" linkage becomes especially clear. In Beijing's narrative, Taiwan represents both an internal incompleteness and an external vulnerability. It is the most important remaining territorial question associated with China's long era of fragmentation, and it is the place where foreign pressure is most easily read through the lens of historical injury. (For how public opinion is prepared for these positions through patriotic education, commemorative law, and state media, see Part 2 of this series.)

That helps explain the unusual rigidity of Chinese rhetoric on Taiwan. In many other areas of foreign policy, Beijing can calibrate, delay, or blur its positions. On Taiwan, it rarely does. Ambiguity would imply toleration of permanent incompleteness. But the rejuvenation narrative insists that the era of weakness and division must end.

Peter Hays Gries's work on Chinese nationalism is helpful here. Gries argues that narratives of humiliation, pride, and national injury have deeply shaped modern Chinese political identity and diplomacy.9 Once the nation is imagined as recovering from humiliation, visible compromise on sovereignty becomes politically fraught. It can be framed not as prudence, but as betrayal—proof that China has still not fully overcome the weakness of its past.

Military modernization as the material answer to historical vulnerability

If Taiwan is the clearest legal and symbolic expression of the doctrine, military modernization is the instrument meant to make it credible.

The 2019 defense white paper, China's National Defense in the New Era, presents the international environment in stark terms. It states that the world is "not yet a tranquil place" and argues that China's security risks "should not be overlooked."10 It then concludes that "greater efforts have to be invested in military modernization."11

That wording matters. The white paper does not portray modernization as a prestige project or simply as the normal privilege of a richer country. It portrays modernization as necessary because the international system remains competitive, uncertain, and potentially hostile. In other words, sovereignty without deterrent capacity is not enough.

Here again, the connection to the memory of humiliation is strong. If the old lesson is that China was bullied because it was weak, then strength becomes a precondition for dignity. Military modernization is not just about winning wars. It is about ensuring that no outside power can again impose terms on China, exploit its territorial disputes, or prevent what Beijing calls national rejuvenation.

The doctrine is fundamentally preventative. No partition. No externally imposed settlement. No return to strategic helplessness.

This logic also appears in Xi Jinping's July 1, 2021 speech marking the centenary of the CCP. Xi declared that the Chinese people would "never allow any foreign force to bully, oppress, or subjugate us," warning that anyone who tried would collide with a "great wall of steel."12

The rhetoric is dramatic, but its significance is analytical. "Bully," "oppress," and "subjugate" are the verbs of the humiliation narrative. The "great wall of steel" is the answer: a modernized military, a stronger state, and a society organized around resistance to coercion.

Anti-hegemonism and the refusal of containment

This same framework shapes Chinese diplomacy more broadly.

Beijing's rhetoric about "hegemonism," "containment," and foreign interference can sometimes sound like standard great-power defensiveness. But when placed in the historical frame the PRC itself uses, it becomes more specific. It reflects opposition to any international order in which stronger states can dictate terms to China, exploit Chinese vulnerabilities, or deny it equal standing.

That is why the Taiwan white paper's language about "external forces" is so important. It does more than criticize U.S. policy. It places present-day competition into a longer historical pattern: outsiders using pressure at China's edge to constrain China's future.13

In that sense, anti-hegemonism is not merely ideological branding. It is a historically charged diplomatic posture. Resistance to coercion is cast as resistance to the reappearance of humiliation. Strategic autonomy is cast as dignity restored.

Counterarguments: realism and domestic nationalism

There are, of course, alternative explanations.

A realist could argue that China's behavior needs no humiliation framework at all. Any rising great power facing rivalry with the United States, unresolved territorial disputes, and dependence on maritime access would care intensely about Taiwan, deterrence, and military modernization.

A domestic-politics argument would add that nationalism is useful to the regime. Historical grievance can mobilize public support, legitimize firmness, and raise the political costs of compromise.

Both arguments are persuasive as far as they go. But neither fully explains the language Beijing uses. Realism explains why sovereignty matters. It does not explain why sovereignty is framed in such morally absolute, historically saturated terms. Domestic politics explains why the state amplifies the humiliation narrative. It does not explain why that narrative remains so central to elite conceptions of security and order.

The stronger interpretation is that humiliation memory and material strategy work together. Strategic rivalry shapes the stakes. Historical memory shapes the meaning.

Why sovereignty becomes security

Seen in this light, the PRC's approach to Taiwan, territorial integrity, and military modernization is best understood as an effort to prevent the return of a historically remembered condition. The Anti-Secession Law defines division as intolerable. The Taiwan white paper defines reunification as necessary for rejuvenation and foreign involvement as containment. The defense white paper defines the international environment as dangerous enough to demand accelerated modernization. Xi's rhetoric translates all of this into a public promise that China will never again be bullied or subjugated.

This is why sovereignty is treated as security.

For Beijing, a divided China is a vulnerable China, and a vulnerable China is one step closer to the world that produced humiliation in the first place. That is the underlying logic connecting historical memory to contemporary doctrine. And it points directly to the next issue in this series: if the lesson of humiliation is that vulnerability invites coercion, then economic and technological self-reliance will also be treated not as optional development policy, but as a core part of national security.
