Development as Immunity: Industrial Policy, Tech Self-Reliance, and the Anti-Humiliation Growth Model

In Xi-era China, development is not just an economic objective. It is a sovereignty instrument: a way to reduce dependence, harden the country against coercion, and ensure that China is never again exposed by the forms of weakness associated with the Century of Humiliation.

Part 4 of 5 — The Century of Humiliation and Chinese Government Policy

In Xi-era China, "development" is not just an economic objective. It is increasingly presented as a sovereignty instrument: a way to reduce dependence, harden the country against coercion, and ensure that China is never again exposed by the forms of weakness associated with the Century of Humiliation. That is what makes contemporary Chinese industrial policy different from a standard growth strategy. The governing logic is not simply richer is better. It is stronger is safer. In that frame, factories, supply chains, semiconductors, data systems, and domestic demand are not only engines of prosperity. They are protections against vulnerability.

That logic is stated with unusual clarity in Xi Jinping's 2012 "Road to Rejuvenation" speech, delivered at one of the Communist Party's most symbolically charged historical exhibitions. Reflecting on the period since the Opium War, Xi argued that "backwardness left us vulnerable to attack, whereas only development makes us strong."1 This is more than a slogan. It is a theory of history translated into statecraft. The problem of modern China, in this telling, was not foreign aggression in the abstract, but the material weakness that made foreign aggression effective. The answer, therefore, is not merely remembrance. It is capability. Development becomes a form of historical insurance: the means by which the state intends to prevent a recurrence of dependency, subordination, and national humiliation.

That fusion of development and protection has also been written into law. The National Security Law of the People's Republic of China states in Article 1 that the law is formulated in part to "realize the great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation," while Article 8 says explicitly that safeguarding national security "shall be coordinated with economic and social development."2 Those phrases matter because they collapse what outside observers often treat as separate domains. Growth is not outside the security realm; it is built into it. The law's broad conception of security includes economic, technological, resource, and social dimensions, suggesting that national security is not just about borders and armies but about whether the country possesses the capabilities to withstand external pressure and sustain its own development trajectory.

This is the background against which Xi's emphasis on technological self-reliance should be understood. In 2021, he said that "sci-tech self-reliance and self-strengthening should always be considered a strategic support for national development."3 The wording is revealing. Science and technology are not described merely as drivers of productivity or prestige; they are "strategic support." In other words, they are load-bearing assets for the nation's future. In a system shaped by the memory of foreign encroachment, dependence on external technology is not just inefficient. It is dangerous. A state that cannot secure core technologies for itself remains exposed to embargoes, sanctions, chokepoints, and political leverage from abroad.

That is also why the language of innovation in China has become increasingly tied to self-reliance. In DigiChina's lexicon on zizhu chuangxin (自主创新), the term is described as a "central strategic concept" in Xi-era rhetoric, built on a longer tradition of emphasizing self-reliance in industry and technology.4 This is a useful reminder that China's current technology push is not simply a reactive response to the most recent round of U.S. export controls, though those restrictions have clearly reinforced it. It is part of a deeper political tradition in which national strength depends on minimizing reliance on outsiders for capabilities judged essential to sovereignty. What changes under Xi is the scale and centrality of that principle. Innovation is no longer just an upgrade path. It is a defensive imperative.

The clearest expression of that imperative in industrial policy is Made in China 2025. As the CSET translation notes, the strategy aims both to raise the competitiveness of Chinese manufacturing and to "reduce China's dependence on foreign manufactured goods."5 But the document's own framing is even more revealing. Manufacturing is described as "the mainstay of the national economy," "an instrument of rejuvenation," and the foundation of national strength and security. It also says China's manufacturing base remains "large but not strong," with serious weaknesses in "independent innovation" and continued dependence on foreign countries for key and core technologies and high-end equipment.6 That language is striking because it directly links industrial weakness to strategic vulnerability. Made in China 2025 is therefore not only a plan for modernization. It is a "never again" document: an attempt to ensure that the country's economic foundations cannot be held hostage by external powers at moments of stress.

The same pattern appears in the state's promotion of "dual circulation." In the official Xinhua explainer carried by the State Council website, China's "dual circulation" development pattern is presented as a way to sustain growth by strengthening both domestic demand and foreign trade in order to meet "challenges in a new development stage."7 Domestic circulation is the mainstay, while domestic and overseas markets reinforce one another. This is not a doctrine of simple autarky. China is not renouncing global trade. Rather, it is trying to rebalance globalization on terms that leave it less exposed. Stronger internal demand, more resilient domestic production, and smoother internal circulation all reduce the risk that external shocks can derail national development. An Asian Development Bank note on the 14th Five-Year Plan makes the same point in more neutral language, observing that the plan emphasizes innovation as the core of modern development and relies on dual circulation as the growth paradigm.8 Together, these texts suggest that resilience has become a defining criterion of economic policy.

Once development is defined in those terms, the securitization of economic governance becomes easier to understand. The Data Security Law says in Article 1 that it was formulated to standardize data handling, ensure data security, promote data development and use, and safeguard "national sovereignty, security, and development interests."9 Article 13 then says the state will "coordinate overall development and security" in the data domain.10 The implication is straightforward: data is not just a commercial resource. It is a strategic asset. Governance of data, then, is not framed merely as regulation for market efficiency or privacy administration, but as part of a larger effort to secure the nation's development path. In this framework, control over data flows, platforms, and digital infrastructure becomes part of the same anti-vulnerability project that animates industrial and technology policy.

The Cybersecurity Law makes the same move. Article 1 says the law is intended to "ensure cybersecurity," "safeguard cyberspace sovereignty and national security," and promote "the healthy development of the informatization of the economy and society."11 Article 3 adds that the state "equally" stresses cybersecurity and informatization development.12 Again, the point is not just that security concerns shape digital governance. It is that development and security are written into the same legal architecture. The digital economy is to grow, but in a way that is politically supervised, territorially anchored, and insulated from strategic dependence. This is why China's contemporary policy regime is best understood not simply as developmental, and not simply as securitized, but as a developmental security state.

There are, of course, counterarguments. Industrial policy is not uniquely Chinese, and resilience language is now common across major economies. The United States, the European Union, Japan, and others all speak more openly than they once did about supply-chain security, critical technologies, and strategic industries. It is also fair to ask whether "humiliation" functions partly as retrospective moral framing for policies that would likely exist anyway under conditions of great-power competition. That caution is worth keeping. The Century of Humiliation is not a sufficient explanation for every Chinese economic decision. But it remains a highly revealing one, because official discourse repeatedly returns to the same structure of argument: backwardness produced vulnerability; vulnerability invited coercion; therefore development must be organized to reduce dependence and harden the country against pressure.13 The historical memory is not incidental. It supplies the emotional and ideological grammar through which policy is explained and legitimized.

Seen this way, China's current economic model is not simply growth-first nationalism with some security language layered on top. It is an anti-humiliation growth model: a theory of national protection built out of manufacturing capacity, technological independence, domestic-market depth, and control over data and networks. Development is treated as immunity. And once development itself is redefined as a security project, it becomes easier for the state to argue that broader forms of political supervision and social control are also necessary to prevent vulnerability before it appears. That is the bridge to the final essay in the series.
