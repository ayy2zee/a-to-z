Things I Learned Building My First Side Project

I recently shipped the first side project I've ever taken from idea to something actually usable by other people. It wasn't pretty, and it took way longer than it should have. Here's what I took away from it.

Start ugly

I spent the first two weeks making it look good before it did anything. That was a mistake. No one cares what a product looks like if it doesn't work. I should have built the ugliest possible version that solved the core problem, then cleaned it up. Instead I polished something that didn't exist yet.

Scope is the enemy

My original idea had seven features. The thing I shipped had two. Every feature I cut made the project more likely to actually get finished. The discipline isn't in building more — it's in saying no to your own ideas long enough to ship the thing.

Show people early

I waited until it felt "ready" to show anyone. That was six weeks in. Within 20 minutes of sharing it with three people, I discovered that the most important feature I'd built was the one nobody wanted, and the one thing I'd considered an afterthought was what everyone asked about first.

Shipping is a muscle

The hardest part wasn't the code. It was pressing the button. There's a particular anxiety that comes with putting something you made into the world — what if it's bad? what if nobody cares? — and the only cure is doing it anyway, repeatedly, until it stops feeling so heavy.

I'm already two weeks into the next one. This time I'm starting with the ugliest possible version.
