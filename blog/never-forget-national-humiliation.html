Never Forget National Humiliation: Patriotic Education, Museums, and the Politics of Memory

Part 2 of 5 — The Century of Humiliation and Chinese Government Policy

It is tempting to treat historical memory as a matter of rhetoric, symbolism, or online outrage. In contemporary China, that misses the point. The politics of the Century of Humiliation are not only expressed in speeches or nationalist moods; they are built into institutions. They appear in curricula, legal mandates, museums, commemorative calendars, media systems, and now internet governance. That is why memory politics in the People's Republic should be understood as policy. The state does not merely remember humiliation. It organizes how humiliation is to be taught, where it is to be displayed, when it is to be ritually performed, and which political conclusions citizens are meant to draw from it.

The Post-Tiananmen Turn

The crucial move came in the post-Tiananmen era, when patriotic education was elevated from a general slogan into a governing project. The 1994 patriotic education outline made modern Chinese history central to patriotic education and emphasized teaching the nation's resistance to foreign invasion and oppression, national unity, and opposition to separatism. In other words, the state made a specific reading of modern history into a civic formation strategy. Suisheng Zhao's classic account remains useful here: he argues that patriotic education was part of a "state-led nationalist movement" that helped the post-1989 leadership rebuild legitimacy on a non-Communist ideological basis. Zheng Wang later pushed the argument further, showing how the CCP's use of history education and memory sites worked to reestablish legitimacy, consolidate national identity, and shape political discourse after the Cold War. The point was not just to remember the past, but to govern the present through the past.

That helps explain why the Century of Humiliation remains so politically useful. A memory of national injury can do several things at once. It can define the nation morally as a victim that endured foreign predation. It can define the Party politically as the force that ended that vulnerability. And it can define contemporary dangers—separatism, territorial encroachment, foreign pressure, ideological subversion—as echoes of an older pattern rather than isolated disputes. The result is a historical narrative with clear policy implications: unity must be protected, sovereignty must be defended, and vigilance must be normalized. What looks like identity formation is also regime maintenance.

The Xi Era: Deepening the Architecture

The Xi era did not invent this architecture, but it has deepened, broadened, and formalized it. In the official 2019 outline on patriotic education in the "new era," patriotic education is explicitly tied to the "Chinese Dream of national rejuvenation." The document says patriotic education should take that rejuvenation as its theme, ensure that love for Party, country, and socialism "go hand in hand," and guide people to safeguard national reunification and ethnic unity. It also says the work should focus on the young and that patriotism should be "embedded in the whole process of school education." This is not passive remembrance. It is a program for building citizens whose political emotions, historical consciousness, and national identity are aligned with the project of rejuvenation.

The 2019 outline also makes clear that the transmission belt is much wider than the classroom. It calls for better use of mass media, literary and artistic works, and online patriotic content to create a favorable atmosphere for patriotic education. That matters because it shows how memory governance has moved beyond textbooks into an integrated information environment. Schooling remains central, but it is supplemented by media circulation, cultural production, and digital distribution. Patriotic education is thus designed not as a single lesson but as an ecosystem. The message of humiliation and rejuvenation should meet citizens in class, on screens, in museums, and in commemorative rituals.

Legal Codification: The 2023 Patriotic Education Law

The most important Xi-era shift, though, is legal codification. China's Patriotic Education Law, adopted in October 2023 and effective January 1, 2024, turns what had long been a campaign logic into a statutory framework. Article 3 says patriotic education should take "building China into a modern socialist country in all respects and advancing the rejuvenation of the Chinese nation on all fronts" as a "striking theme," while emphasizing national unity and ethnic solidarity. Article 15 requires all levels and types of schools to have patriotic education "permeate the entire course of school education" and integrate it into subjects across the curriculum. Article 32 extends that reach into the digital sphere by requiring internet information service providers to strengthen online patriotic education content and produce and disseminate patriotic works. With the law, memory politics is no longer just encouraged; it is assigned, distributed, and administered.

That legal framework also reveals something deeper about the regime's conception of politics. The law does not treat patriotism as a feeling that emerges spontaneously from society. It treats patriotism as something to be organized through institutions with designated responsibilities: schools, families, state agencies, cultural organizations, memorial venues, and digital platforms. Article 26 directs patriotic education bases and museums to strengthen content construction and enrich displays. Article 28 requires local governments to organize memorial activities on dates such as the anniversary of victory in the War of Resistance and the National Memorial Day for the victims of the Nanjing Massacre. In this sense, the law transforms memory into public administration. It gives collective remembrance bureaucratic form.

Museums and Commemorative Days

Museums are central to this project because they convert narrative into space. The National Museum of China explicitly lists "The Road to Rejuvenation" and "The Road to Rejuvenation: New Era" among its permanent exhibitions. That matters not simply because of what these exhibits say, but because of what they do. They materialize a state-approved temporal arc: suffering, resistance, salvation, revival. Visitors do not just read about national humiliation; they move through it physically as a curated story that culminates in rejuvenation under Party leadership. Museums, then, are not decorative supplements to patriotic education. They are pedagogical infrastructure—places where history is staged as civic truth.

Commemorative days function similarly. In 2014, the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress ratified September 3 as the day marking victory in the war against Japanese aggression and December 13 as the national memorial day for victims of the Nanjing Massacre. Those decisions demonstrate that remembrance itself is a matter of legislation. The state is not merely allowing memory to circulate; it is placing memory on the calendar and requiring it to recur. A memorial day is a schedule for emotion. It tells schools, media outlets, local governments, and citizens when grief, warning, gratitude, and vigilance are supposed to be publicly enacted.

Memory as Political Technology

This is why the Century of Humiliation exerts policy influence so directly. It supplies legitimacy, mobilization, and threat perception all at once. Zhao showed that patriotic education helped replace a fading communist ideological core with nationalist legitimacy. Wang argues that the CCP's historical reeducation of the public helped justify one-party rule and shape foreign-policy discourse. William Callahan adds another layer by arguing that national humiliation discourse is not just passive victimhood but "a very active notion of history and recovery." That insight is especially important. The narrative does not tell citizens only that China was wronged. It tells them that recovery from humiliation requires action: unity, discipline, vigilance, territorial integrity, and suspicion toward any force that appears to reopen old vulnerabilities.

Seen this way, patriotic education is not reducible to anti-foreign resentment. It is better understood as a political technology that links domestic order to historical redemption. The Party presents itself as the agent that ended the era of humiliation, and therefore as the institution uniquely capable of preventing its return. National unity becomes more than an administrative priority; it becomes a moral lesson drawn from the past. Sovereignty disputes become more than strategic disputes; they become tests of whether China has truly escaped its history of weakness. Memory thereby shapes foreign-policy psychology even before any specific crisis occurs. It prepares the public to read pressure from abroad through a script of injury, resistance, and restoration.

A Counterargument Worth Taking Seriously

There is, however, an important counterargument. Not all Chinese nationalism is top-down, and not all public reception can be read straight off the state's intentions. Online nationalism can outrun official messaging, local experiences can vary, and citizens do not absorb propaganda identically. Callahan is right to warn against overly deterministic readings of humiliation discourse, and Zhao himself analyzed not only the campaign's contents but also its methods and effectiveness rather than assuming perfect state control. So the strongest version of the argument is not that the state manufactures every nationalist sentiment from above. It is that the state has built the institutional channels through which some memories become authoritative, recurrent, and politically usable. Even when reception varies, the architecture remains top-down. Curriculum, law, museums, and commemorations set the agenda on which public nationalism then operates.

Memory Made Governable

That is the central lesson of patriotic education in contemporary China. The Century of Humiliation matters not simply because it is remembered, but because it has been made governable. It is embedded in schools, formalized in law, staged in museums, repeated in ritual calendars, and amplified through media and internet platforms. Memory here is not the opposite of policy. Memory is one of policy's instruments. And that is what makes this history so consequential for the present: once humiliation is institutionalized as a civic lesson, it becomes available for continual reuse in questions of unity, legitimacy, sovereignty, and security.

That is also where the next essay naturally begins—at the point where remembered humiliation stops being mainly pedagogical and becomes operationalized as threat perception.
